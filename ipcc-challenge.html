Net zero emissions, Table of major conclusions in different IPCC reports on page 281 and thereafter

  1.     Net zero CO2 emissions as the conditions in which anthropogenic CO2 emissions are balanced by anthropogenic CO2 removels over a specified period (Page 242).
  2.     When metric-weighted anthropogenic emissions are balanced by correspoinding removals, it’s referred as Net zero GHG emissions (Page 242).
  3.     Achieving net zero globally at any given time doesn’t imply that individual entity have to reach net zero at the same time or even at all (Page 242).
  4.     For GWP100, net zero GHG emissions would generally imply a peak in global surface temperatures followed by a gradual decline (Page 242).
  5.     For virtually all scenarios in IPCC, CDR is necessary to reach global net zero co2 and net zero GHG emissions (Page 242).
  6.     Net negative anthropogenic GHG emissions may become necessary to stabilize global temperatures in long term, should climate feedbacks further affect natural GHG sources and sinks (Page 242).
  7.     CDR can be achieved through afforestation, reforestation, soil carbon management, biochar, direct air capture, carbon capture and storage (Page 242).
  8.     Scientific climate research has doubled in output in every 5-6 years. This sheer volume of publications presents a challenge for a comprehensive, robust and transparent assessment (Page 243).
  9.     WG1 chapters has still not included indigenous and local knowledge in their assessment findings (Page 243).
  10. It’s likely that future changes will continue to show largest signals in the high northern latitudes, but with most apparent warming in the tropics because of small variability (Page 243).
  11. It is not necessary that size of change is most important. Rate of change or size of change of natural variability to which ecosystems are adapted are also important (Page 243).
  12. Ocean warming accounts for 91% of heating of the climate system, land warming 5%, ice loss 3% and atmospheric warming only 1% (Page 283).

History of Scenarios

  1.     In the IPCC reports, the starting time of scenarios moves as actual emissions supersede earlier emission assumptions (Page 237).
  2.     By 2000, the IPCC Special Report on Emission Scenarios produced SRES scenarios, albeit without assuming any climate policy-induced mitigation (Page 237).
  3.     The four broad groups of SRES scenarios – A1, A2, B1, and B2 – were the first scenarios to emphasize socio-economic storylines, and also the first to emphasize other GHGs, land-use change, and aerosols (Page 237).
  4.     Then the RCP scenarios came and provided low-emission pathways that implied strong climate mitigation, including an example with negative CO2 emissions on large scale, i.e. RCP2.6 (Page 238).
  5.     Building on SRES, RCP scenarios included a time series of emissions and concentrations of the full suite of the GHGs, aerosols, and chemically active gases as well as land use and land cover (Page 238).
  6.     Extended concentration pathways (ECPs) describe extensions of the RCP from 2100 to 2300, however, these do not represent fully consistent scenarios (Page 238).
  7.     The new set of SSP scenarios now features higher top-level Co2 emissions although the most significant change is the addition of very low climate change mitigation scenario SSP1-1.9 compared to RCP2.6 (Page 238).
  8.     Previously, none of the scenario sets featured a scenario that involves a very pronounced peak and decline emissions trajectory but SSP1-1.9 does so now (Page 238).
  9.     SSP scenarios feature a later peak of global emissions for the lower scenarios simply as a consequence of historical emissions not having followed the trajectory projected by previous low scenarios (Page 238).
  10. When the SRES scenario first appeared the debate was often whether the scenario overestimates actual world emission developments (Page 238).
  11. With strong emissions increase throughout 2000, the debate then shifted to whether the lower future climate change mitigation scenarios were rendered unfeasible (Page 238).
  12. The global fuel and industrial CO2 emissions of recent decades tracked approximately the middle of the projected scenario ranges, through regional differences (Page 238).
  13. When exploring different climate futures, scenarios with no or no additional climate policies are referred to as baseline or reference scenarios, SSP3-7.0 and SSP5-8.5 are explicit no climate policy scenarios (page 238).
  14. The uncertainty range on carbon cycle feedback includes stronger feedback than assumed in the default derivation of RCP8.5 and SSP5-8.5 concentrations (Page 239).
  15. Short-term emissions trends alone do not rule out an opposite trend in the future (Page 239).
  16. Some regional climate effects can be considered almost scenario independent for a given level of warming (Page 239).
  17. Global Warming Levels are expressed in terms of global surface air temperatures (Page 239). Not land temperatures!
  18. Much of the impact analysis is based on previous scenarios (RCP or SRES) and climate change mitigation analysis is based on new emissions scenarios in addition to the main SSP scenarios (Page 239).
  19. Climate impacts in a transient world relate to a scenario in which the world continues to warm (Page 239).
  20. Climate impact at the same warming level can also be estimated from equilibrium states after a short-term stabilization by the end of the 21st century or long-term stabilization from multidecadal to multi-millennial timescales (Page 239).
  21. While the global warming level is a good proxy for the climate state, it does not uniquely define a change in the global or regional climate state (Page 240).
  22. The dominance of CO2 compared to other well-mixed GHGs allows policymakers to make use of the carbon budget concept in which GWP-weighted combinations of multiple GHGs are used to define emission targets (Page 240).
  23. Rapid emissions followed by steep cuts and potential net negative emissions would be characterized by a higher maximum warming and faster warming rate compared with the same cumulative CO2 emissions spread over a longer period (Page 240).

SSP scenarios, Table on details of SSP scenarios on Page 233

  1.     SSP scenarios can be used for concentration-driven or emission-driven experiments. Emission-driven (Page 230).
  2.     ESMs can be run with emissions and concentrations data for GHGs and aerosols, land use or landcover maps, and calculate the level of radiative forcings internally (Page 230).
  3.     The radiative forcing labels, such as 8.5 or 2.6 in RCP or SSP, are thus approximate labels for the year 2100 only (Page 230).
  4.     One of the key advances in SSP scenarios as compared to the RCP is the wider span of assumptions on future air-quality mitigation measures and emissions of short-lived climate forcers (Page 231).
  5.     One limitation of the SSP scenarios is they reduce emissions from all major ozone-depleting substances uniformly, rather than representing a fuller range of possible high and low emissions futures (Page 231).
  6.     The SSP and RCP are not directly comparable. The gas-to-gas compositions differ, the projected 21st century may also differ, and the overall effective radiative forcing differs and tend to be higher in the SSP as compared to the RCP (Page 231).
  7.     The difference in the SSP and RCP scenarios is partially due to different scenario characteristics rather than different ESM characteristics only (Page 231).
  8.     More than 1000 scenarios were assessed in the WGIII report (Page 231).
  9.     WGIII also considers various mitigation policies through so-called illustrative pathways (Page 231).
  10. No likelihood is attached to the scenarios in the WG1 report (Page 231).
  11. The possibility of high emission scenarios, such as SSP5-8.5 has been debated in light of the recent developments in the energy sector (Page 231).
  12. SSP scenarios fill certain gaps identified in the RCPs, such as scenario assuming reduced air pollution control and thus higher aerosol emissions was missing from the RCPs (Page 232).
  13. Similarly, a very strong mitigation scenario in line with the 1.5-degree goal was also missing from the RCPs, and the SSP1-1.9 scenario now fills this gap (Page 232).
  14. While Integrated Assessment models produce internally consistent future time series of certain GHGs for the SSP scenarios, these emissions scenarios undergo several processing steps for harmonization and in-filling before also being complemented by several datasets so that ESMs can run these SSPs (Page 234).
  15. Most of the CMIP6 ESM experiments are concentration-driven runs (Page 234).
  16. The emission-driven experiments use the same input datasets as concentration-driven ESM experiments, except that they use CO2 emissions rather than the concentration (Page 234).
  17. In emission-driven experiments, atmospheric CO2 concentrations are calculated internally using ESM interactive carbon cycle module and thus differ from the prescribed default CO2 concentrations (Page 234).

Initialized ensembles, ESMval tool, Emergent Constraints, SSP scenarios

  1.     Tiny changes in initial conditions lead to different evolutions for the individual realizations of the system as a whole. This type of initial condition ensemble is referred as ‘micro’ ensembles (Page 222).
  2.     Other experiments start from a set of well-separated ocean initial conditions to sample the uncertainty in the circulation state of the ocean and its role on the longer scale variations. This is referred as the ‘macro’ perturbation ensembles (Page 222).
  3.     Initialized ensembles can also be used to evaluate climate model parameterizations if models are initialized appropriately (Page 222).
  4.     For the historical simulations in CMIP6, all models use common forcings. Depending on the model setup these include emissions and concentrations of short lived species, long lived green house gases, biomass burning emissions, global gridded land-use forcing data, solar forcing and stratospheric aerosol data from volcanoes (Page 224).
  5.     The ESMval tool includes variety of diagnostics and performance metrics relevant for coupled Earth system processes such as mean, variability and trends, emergent constraints (Page 224).
  6.     Another evaluation tool is CLIVAR 2020 metric package (Page 224).
  7.     Instrument simulators provide estimates of what a satellite would see if looking down on model simulated planet. This improves the direct comparison of modelled variables such as cloud or precipitation with observations from the satellite (Page 225).
  8.     An emergent constraint is the relationship between an uncertainty aspect of future climate change and observable feature of the Earth system (Page 225).
  9.     Where an ensemble of different ESMs displays a relationship between short term observable variance with longer term sensitivity, an observation of the short term variation in the real world can be converted, via the model-based relationship, into an emergent constraint on the sensitivity (Page 226).
  10. Emergent constraints use the spread in model projections to estimate the sensitivities of the climate system to anthropogenic forcing, providing another type of ensemble-wode information that is not readily available from simulations with one ESM alone (Page 226).
  11. Indiscriminate data-mining of the multi-dimensional outputs from ESMs can lead to spurious correlations and less-than-robust emergent constraints on future changes (Page 226).
  12. Emergent constraints also represent an implicit weighting technique that explicitly links present performance to future projections (Page 226).
  13. Model independence has been defined in terms of performace differences within an ensemble however this definition is sensitive to choice of variable, observational dataset, metric, time period etc. Perforamce ranked ensemble is sometimes shown to perform worse than random ensemble (Page 226).
  14. The adequacy of the constraint provided by the data and experimental methods can be tested using a calibration-validation style partitioning of observations into two sets, or a perfect model approach where one of the ensemble members is treated as the reference dataset and all model weights are calibrated against it (Page 226).
  15. Scenarios are not predictions, instead they provide a what-if investigation of the implications of various developments and actions (Page 227).
  16. SSP scenarios are widely used to refer directly to future emissions and concentration scenarios that result from combining these socio-economic development pathaways with climate mitigation assumptions (Page 228).
  17. The SSP narratives and drivers used to develop scenarios of energy use, air pollution control, land use and GHS developments using intergrated assessment models (IAM) (Page 230).
  18. An IAM can derive multiple emissions futures for each SSP pathaway assuming no new mitigation policies or various levels of additional mitigation action (Page 230).
  19. By design, the evolution of drivers and emissions within the SSP scenario do not take into account the effects of climate change (Page 230).
  20. The SSP scenario categorization, focusing on end-of-century radiative forcing levels, reflects how scenarios were conceptualized until recently, namely, to reach a particular climate target in 2100 at lowest cost and irrespective of whether the target was exceeded over the century (Page 230).

Intermediate complexity models and emission metrics

  1.     Earth system models of intermediate complexity (EMICs) are simplified models which include processes in a more parameterized way and generally have lower spatial resolution than ESMs (Page 219).
  2.     EMICs require much lesser computational resource and can be integrated for 1000 of years (Page 219).
  3.     Examples of EMIC include zonally average or mixed layer ocean models coupled to statistical-dynamical models, to low-resolution three dimensional ocean models coupled to simplified dynamical models of the atmosphere (Page 219).
  4.     EMIC community focuses more on the specific research questions and develop individual models accordingly (Page 219).
  5.     Physical emulators are class of heavily parameterized models designed to reproduce responses of more complex, process-based models and provide rapid translations of emissions via concentrations and radiative forcings, into probabilistic estimates of changes to the physical climate system (Page 219).
  6.     Simple climate models are low-dimensional models of the energy balance, radiative transfer, carbon cycle or combination of these (Page 219).
  7.     Simple climate models can be tuned to reproduce the calculations of climate mean variables of a given ESM, assuming that their uncertainties across process-oriented responses (Page 219).
  8.     When simple climate models run in emulation mode, they are termed as emulators (Page 219).
  9.     Emissions metrics compare the radiative forcing, temperature change or other climate effects arising from emissions of CO2 against those from emissions of non-CO2 radiative forcing agents (such as CH4 or N2O) (Page 220)
  10. The Global Warming Potential (GWP) is one of the emission metrics that integrate the calculated radiative forcing contribution following an idealized pulse emission over a chosen time horizon (Page 220).
  11. The global temperature change potential (GTP) which considers the contribution of emissions to the global mean temperature at a specific time after emission (Page 220).
  12. Global Precipitation change potential is used to quantify precipitation change per unit mass of emission of a given forcing agent (Page 220).
  13. Termed GWP and combined global temperature change potential, these metrics allow the construction of a near-linear relationship between global surface temperature change and cumulative CO2 and CO2 eq emissions of both short and long lived forcing agents (Page 221).
  14. The broader availability of large ensembles may allow for novel tests of fitness that better account for natural climate variability (Page 221).

Data assimilation, reanalysis, advances and gaps in CMIP6 models, model tuning.

  1. Reanalysis are model outputs constrained by observations using data assimilation techniques, but the term has also been used to describe observation-based produced by using simpler statistical methods (Page 212).
  2. For reanalysis, observational issues include a lack of underlying observations in some regions, changes in observational system over time, time dependent errors, errors in boundary conditions (Page 213).
  3. Assimilation of sparse or inconsistent observations can introduce mass or energy imbalances (Page 213).
  4. Precipitation is not usually assimilated in reanalysis and depending on the region, reanalysis precipitation can differ from observations by more than the observational error (Page 213).
  5. Atmospheric 20th century reanalysis assimilates only surface and sea level pressure observations, and is constrained by time-varying observed changes in atmospheric constituents, prescribed sea surface temperatures and sea-ice concentration (Page 214).
  6. Longer reanalysis going back to before instrumental records are being developed and include complete integration of paleoclimate archives and newly available early instrumental data (Page 214).
  7. The improvements between CMIP5 and CMIP6 remains modest (Page 216).
  8. Model performance depends on model formulation and paramaterizations as much as on resolution (Page 216).
  9. The explicit representation of ocean eddies due to increased grid resolution (from one degree to ¼) is a major advance in a number of CMIP6 ocean model (Page 217).
  10. The land surface processes represented vary across CMIP6 models with at least some key processes such as fire, permafrost carbon, microbes, nutrients, vegetation dynamics, plant demography, absent from any particular ESM land model (Page 217).
  11. Whether tuning should be performed to facilitate accurate simulation of long-term trends such as changes in global mean temperature over the historical era or rather be perfomed for each processes independently such that collective behavior is emergent, is an open question (Page 217).
  12. Behaviour of the ESM depends on the tuning strategy (Page 218).
  13. The most important global tuning target is the net TOA heat flux and its radiative components (Page 218).
  14. Other global targets include decomposition of energy fluxes at TOA into a clear sky component and radiative components, global mean air and ocean temperature, sea-ice extent, sea-ice volume, glacial mass balance, and global root mean square of precipitation (Page 218).
  15. Adjustments are made for the aerosol indirect effects, ocean albedo, marine dimethyl sulfide parameterization or cloud properties (Page 218).
  16. Regional tuning targets include the AMOC, Southern Ocean circulation, temperature profiles in ocean basins, regional land properties and precipitations, latitudinal distribution of radiation, spatial constrasts in TOA radiative fluxes or surface fluxes, stationary waves in the Northern Hemisphere (Page 218).
  17. Majority of CMIP6 modeling groups report that they do not tune their models based on observed trends during the historical period nor for ECS (Page 218).

Changes in observational capacity and networks

  1.     Knowledge of fluxes between the atmosphere and the land surface has improved by combining remote sensing and in-situ measurements (Page 209).
  2.     Fluxnet provides eddy covariance measurements of carbon, water, and energy fluxes (Page 209).
  3.     Observations of atmospheric composition have been improved by surface observation networks and aircraft measurements (Page 209).
  4.     GPS, radio occultations and limb soundings provide information on the atmosphere but only upper troposphere and lower stratosphere data are suitable for climate change assessments (Page 209).
  5.     Handwritten weather journals and ship logs continue to improve spatial and temporal coverage of weather/climate data. Citizen science activities have played a crucial role in digitizing past observational records (Page 209).
  6.     Argo is a global network of ~4000 autonomous profiling floats, measuring horizontal and vertical structures of salinity and ocean temperatures (Page 210).
  7.     Most basin scale arrays of moored ocean instruments have expanded since AR5, providing decades-long records of the ocean and atmosphere properties (Page 210).
  8.     A large number of ocean campaigns during the 2015-2016 El Nino enabled the assessment of short-term climate phenomena such as coral bleaching and mortality of months-long ocean heatwaves (Page 211).
  9.     Fewer aircraft campaigns, ship transits, and ocean-observing buoys during the covid pandemic and have reduced onboard observations (Page 212).
  10. Europe deployed more radiosoundings during the Covid-19 pandemic to account for the reduction In data from air traffic (Page 212).
  11. Estimates show that the effect of a reduction in aircraft data assimilation on weather forecasting skill is small (Page 212).
  12. The transition from manual to automatic observations of weather have reduced the spatial coverage of certain measurements (Page 212)
  13. The number of ships contributing to observations has declined and also been a decline in the number of variables recorded by ships, but there is an increase in the quality and time-resolution of some variables (Page 212).
  14. Water vapour produces a weak signal at 23.8 GHz, which is within the range of frequencies of 5G cellular communication. Concerns have been raised about potential leakage from the 5G network into the operating frequencies of passive sensors on existing weather satellites, which can adversely influence their ability to remotely observe water vapour in the atmosphere (Page 212).   15. The severity of coral bleaching and mortality have increased in recent decades, with profound implications for the recovery of coral climate archives from new and existing sites (Page 212).
  16. While internationally coordinated efforts exist for the recovery of past data, no such coordinated effort exists for vulnerable paleoclimate drivers (Page 212).

Discussion on tipping points and aggregation of regions in the IPCC report.

  1.   Abrupt change is defined as a change that takes place substantially faster than rate of change in recent past (Page 202).
  2.   If the abrupt change occurs because the system becomes unstable, such that the rate of change is independent of forcing, then it is referred as tipping point (Page 202).
  3.   Tipping points is the critical threshold beyond which a system reorganizes often abruptly or irreversibly (Page 202).
  4.   There are concerns that anthropogenic emissions can tip the global climate into a perpetual hot state, however there is no evidence of such response in the global scale projections (Page 202).
  5.   Transitions can be prompted by perturbations such as extremes which forces the system outside of its current well of attraction (in stability plot), this is called noise induced tipping (Page 202).
  6.   Transitions can also occur if a critical threshold is exceeded, this is called bifurcation tipping (Page 202). See the nice Fig. 1.17 which illustratively shows these definitions!
  7.   Surprises are the class of low-likelihood events that cannot be predicted yet, e.g. volcanic eruptions, nuclear war, or biological epidemics, which have an impact on climate (Page 203).
  8.   Evolution of global climate trends emerges as the net result of regional phenomena, however average or aggregate estimates do not reflect the intensity, variability or complexity of regional climate changes (Page 206).
  9.   When climate observation data was sparse and limited, the aggregation of climate variables and classification of climate regions was achieved through the consideration of biomes, which led to the traditional vegetation based Koppen climate zones (Page 206).
  10. More recently, over the last decades, the classification of climate regions is done through the interpolation of aggregated global data or through data driven approaches to define regions that are spatially coherent in terms of climate variability (Page 206).
  11. A very detailed classification of climate regions into complex shapes may be useful for evaluation and projections, national boundaries are useful for decision making whereas rectilinear shapes are useful for downscaling or regional climate modeling, so definition of a region is usually a trade-off (Page 206).

Discussion on different types of uncertainties and their key sources

  1.   Historical simulations are constrained by past radiative forcings and future projections are constrained by GHG concentrations, emissions and radiation forcings. Future predictions are in addition constrained by observed initial conditions of the climate system (Page 196).
  2.     Future radiative forcings are uncertain because it depends on choices made by the society – this is considered as scenario uncertainty (Page 196).
  3.     There are also uncertainties in past emissions and radiative forcings. These are particularly important for simulations of paleoclimate time periods (Page 197).
  4.     Historical radiative forcings due to anthropogenic and natural aerosols are less well constrained than the GHG forcings in observations (Page 197).
  5.     Climate response uncertainty implies uncertainty in how the climate will respond to the specified emissions or radiative forcing combinations. (Page 197).
  6.     Climate response uncertainty arise due to our understanding of the key physical processes or how these processes are represented in the models (Page 197).
  7.     Multiple models are used to sample a broader range of climate response uncertainty however models are not entirely independent of each other. Also there are small scale features which cannot be resolved and long time-scale processes are not fully represented (Page 197).
  8.     In addition to anthropogenic factors, there are natural factors contributing to uncertainty such as variations in solar activity and volcanic eruptions. The uncertainty in these factors is not normally considered in future projections (Page 197).
  9.     There are internal variations, such as El Nino Southern Oscillation, which impacts the climate on decadal time scales, particularly regionally (Page 197).
  10. The different sources of uncertainty interacts with each other, such as timing of volcanic eruption may influence Atlantic multidecadal variability, aerosols can influence decadal modes of variability in the Pacific (Page 197).
  11. Internal variability can be quantified and estimated probabilistically but cannot be reduced (Page 197).
  12. Model response uncertainty can be reduced but not amenable to a frequency based interpretation of probability. Bayesian methods are instead considered to quantify the uncertainty (Page 197).
  13. Multimodels can be used to estimate model response uncertainty and multiple pathaways can be used to estimate scenario uncertainty. The unforced component of internal variability can be estimated from individual members of the same climate model (Page 197). Model response uncertainty and climate response uncertainty are used interchangeably?
  14. For the global mean temperatures, the uncertainty is dominated by emissions scenario and model response uncertainty, and role of internal variability is quite small (Page 197).
  15. For long term, emission scenario uncertainty becomes dominant (Page 198).
  16. The relative uncertainty due to internal variability and model uncertainty increase for smaller spatial scales (Page 198)
  17. For regional precipitation, emission scenario uncertainty is often small than model response uncertainty (Page 198).
  18. Greatest risks may not be associated with the most likely outcome (Page 198). This is my favorite quote so far I think!
  19. Scenario storyline refers to the narrative description of one or more scenarios, focusing on their main characteristics, relationship between key driving forces, and dynamics of their evolution (Page 199).
  20. A physical storyline is a self consistent and possible physical trajectory of the climate systems or a weather or climate event (Page 199).
  21. Exposure refers to presence of people or species or infrastructure in places that could be adversely affected (Page 201).
  22. Vulnerability is the propensity of being adversely affected (Page 201).
  23. Hazard is potential of occurrence of any natural or human-induced climate event that can affect humans, species or our surroundings adversely (Page 201).
  24. Impacts are the consequences or outcomes of climate related hazards. It refers to the effects of lives, ecosystems etc. Impacts can be adverse or beneficial (Page 201).
  25. Climate impact-drivers are physical climate conditions, such as rainfall or temperature means, events, extremes that affects society. They can also be adverse or beneficial (Page 201).
  26. Maladaptation is adaptation responses that do not give desired outcome or have negative side effects (Page 201).

Information on the methodology used in the subsequent chapters.

  1.     Using anomalies in place of absolute values has some potential benefits. For E.g. anomalies are representative of much larger scales whereas absolute values can vary over small spatial scales. Seasonality can be removed in climate indicators using anomalies, this helps in distinguishing variability from long-term trends (Page 189).
  2.     The choice of baseline period is important and therefore assessment of the sensitivity of the analysis to the choice of the baseline is desirable (Page 191).
  3.     The uncertainty in future projections of certain climate variables were noted to decrease if using a more recent baseline, especially for the near term (Page 191).
  4.     The World Meteorological Organization (WMO), uses a 30-year period to define climate normals (Page 191).
  5.     For AR6 WG1, the baseline period of 1995-2014 is used to calculate changes in future climate. The same period is also used for the recent or modern past (Page 191).
  6.     The AR6 uses the mean over 1850-1900 to represent the pre-industrial period, this is when the permanent surface observing networks emerged that provide continuous and sufficiently accurate measurements of global scale. Also models use 1850 as their start date for the historical simulations (Page 192).
  7.     The individual ensemble members can show very different decadal trends, both positive and negative (Page 194).
  8.     Detection of a change is defined as the process of demonstrating significant statistical changes in certain aspects of climate, often using spatially aggregating methods that try to maximize the S/N such as fingerprints (Page 196).
  9.     An identified change is detected in observations if the likelihood of occurrence by chance due to internal variability alone is determined to be small, e.g. <10% (Page 196).
  10. Uncertainty in climate simulations include: uncertainty in the forcings, uncertainty in the climate response to forcings, internal or natural variability of the climate, interactions among these sources of uncertainty (Page 196).
  11. Emergence of climate change signal refers to when a change in climate (i.e. trend, the signal) becomes larger than the amplitude of natural variability (noise). This often represented as S/N, i.e. signal to noise ratio, and emergence occurs at a defined threshold of S/N >1 or 2 (Page 196). Discuss the agreement between projections made in the past using climate models and subsequent observations.

  1.     AR6 has narrowed the likely range of Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity to 2.5 – 4 deg Celcius (Page 184).
  2.     There is a close relationship of cumulative total emissions of CO2 and global mean sea temperature response, it is approximately linear (Page 184).
  3.     Stabilizing the anthropogenic influence on global surface temperature requires CO2 emissions and removals to reach net zero once the remaining carbon budget is exhausted (Page 184).
  4.     Significant reductions of warming due to short-lived climate forcers can reduce the level at which temperature stabilizes once CO2 emissions reach net zero (Page 184).
  5.     A large fraction of this change is essentially irreversible on a multicentury time scale, barring large net removal of CO2 from the atmosphere over a sustained period through as yet unavailable technological means (Page 184).
  6.     Stouffer and Manabe (2017) compared projections made in early 1990s with subsequent observations and found projected surface pattern of warming and vertical structure of temperature change in ocean and atmosphere were realistic (Page 184).
  7.     The first assessment report regional projections are broadly consistent with subsequent observations allowing for regional scale climate variability and differences in projected and actual forcings (Page 187).
  8.     Over 1982-2016, marine heatwaves have very likely doubled in frequency and intensity (Page 187).
  9.     No statistically significant trend is observed in the Antarctic sea ice extent overall for the period 1979-2018 (Page 187).
  10. There is an evidence of increase in annual global proportion of category 4 or 5 tropical cyclones in the recent decades (Page 188).
  11. The natural response of land to anthropogenic environmental change caused a net CO2 sink equivalent to ~29% of total CO2 emissions (Page 188).
  12. Changes in land cover have led to both release of CO2 (causing global warming) and increase in surface albedo (causing surface cooling). Resulting net effect of land use changes has small impact on global average surface temperatures over the historical period (Page 188).
  13. The carbon content of Artic and boreal permafrost is almost twice that of the atmosphere (Page 188).
  14. Global warming is likely to reach 1.5 degree between 2030-2052 if it continues to increase at the current rate (Page 188).
  15. Past emissions alone are unlikely to raise temperature above 1.5 deg C above 1850-1900 levels (Page 188).
  16. Climate models project robust differences in regional climate characteristics between present day and warming of 1.5 deg and also between 1.5 and 2 deg (Page 188).
  17. Extreme sea level events that occur 1 per 100 year, are projected to occur at least once per year at many locations by 2050, especially in the tropics (Page 188).
  18. Global mean sea level rise will be ~0.1 m lower with 1.5 degree warming as compared to 2 deg global warming (Page 188).
  19. Extreme ENSO are projected to become more frequent (Page 188).
  20. The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation is expected to weaken over the 21st century but a collapse is deemed very unlikely (Page 189).
  21. Starting from 2018, the remaining carbon budget for a 50-50 chance of limiting global warming to 1.5 deg C is about GtCO2 and for 66-33 chance is 420 GtCO2 (Page 189).
  22. At constant 2017 emissions, these budgets will be depleted by 2032 and 2028 respectively (Page 189).

Concepts and information on radiative forcing imposed by different atmospheric components/species. Impressive hand-annotated figure by Callendar (1938) on Page 179.

  1.     Radiative Drivers or Forcers: The natural and anthropogenic factors responsible for climate change. Positive radiative drivers induce a warming effect and negative drivers induce a cooling effect (Page 178).
  2.     Radiative Forcing: Net change in the energy budget at the top of the atmosphere, resulting from a change in radiative forcer (Page 178).
  3.     The total radiative forcing over a given time interval represents the sum of positive drivers (inducing warming) and negative drivers (inducing cooling) (Page 178).
  4.     AR5 assessed that total radiative forcing has been positive at least since 1850-1900, inducing warming of the climate system (Page 178).
  5.     Water vapor is the most abundant radiatively active gas accounting for almost 75% of the terrestrial greenhouse effect. However, its residence time in the atmosphere is just 8-10 days as its concentration is largely governed by temperature (Page 179).
  6.     GHGs which are non-condensing and have longer residence time serve as control knobs of climate change, and water vapor concentrations act as a feedback (Page 179).
  7.     Absorption/uptake by the oceans, plants, and soils are primary sources of natural CO2 sinks on decadal to centennial timescales (Page 179).
  8.     Aerosols can cause positive or negative radiative forcing, i.e. can lead to warming and cooling (Page 180).
  9.     Aerosols are also the largest single source of uncertainty in estimating total radiating forcing (Page 181).
  10. In the early 20th century, Bjerknes formulated Navier Stokes equations of motion of the atmosphere. Richardson developed a system of numerical weather prediction based on these equations (Page 181).
  11. Since climate models vary along many dimensions, grid type, resolution, and parameterizations, comparing their results requires special techniques (Page 181).
  12. Both CMIP3 and CMIP5 conducted experiments testing the ability of models to reproduce 20th-century global surface temperature trends with and without anthropogenic forcings. Although some individual models failed to achieve this, the mean trend from the multimodel ensemble captured it (Page 182).
  13. Using only natural forcings, a similar multimodel ensemble could not reproduce the observed post-1970 warming at global or regional scale (Page 182).
  14. Fingerprint studies seek to detect specific observation changes that cannot be explained by the natural variability of the climate system alone. They also attribute statistically the proportion of such changes to human influence.

Presents interesting information on the history of observations and advances made till date.

  1.     Instrumental weather observations at Earth’s surface goes back to 17th century, when thermometers and barometers were invented (Page 174).
  2.     National and colonial weather services started building the network of surface stations in the 19th century. In the mid-19th century, the naval services started keeping semi-standardized records of winds, currents, rainfall, air pressure and temperature at sea, initiating the longest continuous quasi-global instrumental records (Page 175).
  3.     By 1900, a patchy weather data-sharing system reached all continents except Antarctica. In 1905, regular compilation of climatological data began for the whole world (Page 175).
  4.     Global land temperature time series for 1880-1935 used fewer than 150 stations, under a pioneering study by Callendar (1938). Study by Brohan et al. (2006) for 1880-2005 used 4300 stations. Rohde et al., (2013) used 36,000 stations for the period 1753-2011, tapping onto some previously unused station data. Recent versions of this dataset now use over 40,000 land stations (Rhode and Hausfather, 2020) (Page 175).
  5.     In the 2000s, adjustments for the bias due to different measurement methods resulted in the major improvement of the SST data (Page 175).
  6.     Using the then available global surface temperature datasets AR5 WG1 assessed that the GMST increased by 0.85 C from 1880 to 2012 (Page 175).
  7.     Each of the three decades following 1980 was successively warmer at the Earth’s surface than any preceding decade since 1850 (Page 175).
  8.     Data at altitude came initially from mountain summits, balloons and kites but systematic observations started after the radiosonde networks in 1940-1950 (Page 175).
  9.     Radiosonde provides the longest continuous records of the atmosphere’s vertical dimensions (Page 175).
  10. Sounding units mounted on earth’s orbiting satellites provides a second data source for vertical dimensions of the atmosphere since 1978 (Page 175).
  11. There are still marked differences between the temperature measurements from surface, radiosonde and satellite, and this is a subject of ongoing research (Page 175).
  12. Arrhenius (1896) calculated that doubling of atmospheric CO2 would produce warming of around 5-6 deg Celsius, however in 1900 new measurements apparently ruled out CO2 as a greenhouse gas due to overlap with the absorption bands of water vapor (Page 175). That’s interesting!
  13. AR5 WG1 highlighted the other CO2 problem, i.e. Ocean acidification by the absorption of ~20-30% of anthropogenic CO2 from the atmosphere (Page 176).
  14. With a heat capacity of ~1000 times > atmosphere, ocean stores vast majority of the energy retained by Earth. Ocean warming accounts for more than 90% of additional energy accumulated by climate between 1971-2010 (Page 176-177).
  15. Meticulous records by Chinese scholars and govt workers, have permitted detailed reconstructions of China’s climate back to 1000 CE (Page 177). Wow!
  16. Peruvian fishermen named the periodic El Nino current in the pacific, which was linked later to the ENSO (Pages 177).
  17. By the early 20th century, laboratory experiments using tree rings, reconstructed precipitation in the historical time and influence of sunspots cycle on the climate change (Page 178).

These pages provide basic information on the terminologies used for uncertainty and likelihood in the IPCC report.

  1.     A meta-analysis of studies found that political orientation and political party identification are the second most important predictors of views on climate change (first is environmental values) (Page 168).
  2.     IPCC definition of confidence: a qualitative measure of validity of the finding based on type, amount, quality and consistency of evidence and degree of agreement between evidence (Page 169)
  3.     IPCC definition of likelihood: a quantitative measure of uncertainty  in a finding, expressed probabilistically (Page 169)
  4.     Throughout the report, uncertainty is quantified using 90% uncertainty intervals. The 90% intervals, reported in square brackets [x to y], gives the 90% chance of the value to be in this interval. There is still a 10% chance of value to be below x and above y (Page 170).
  5.     When uncertainties are large, researchers may choose to report a wide range as very likely, even though it is less informative about the impacts. In contrast, a high likelihood with narrow uncertainty range may be more informative, yet can be less reliable if there is new research/evidence later that widens the uncertainty range (Page 172).
  6.     The difference between narrow and wide uncertainty range has been confusing to the readers, who often infer wide range to be less certain (Page 172).
  7.     The phrase regional climate information refers to the predefined reference sets of land and ocean regions, various other typological domains (e.g. monsoon, mountains), time periods including baseline periods, near term (2021-2040), medium term (2041-2060) and long term (2081-2100) and global warming levels (Page 172).
  8.     Climate services focus on user specific needs for climate information but most people learn about the climate science findings from the media coverage (Page 173).
